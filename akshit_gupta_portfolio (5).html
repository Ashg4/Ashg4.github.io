A quantitative study of performance-per-dollar across 120 NBA player seasons, building a composite Value Score to surface undervalued contracts using Python, Pandas, and regression analysis.

120Player Records

Background

The Question

NBA teams collectively spend over $4 billion per season on player salaries. Yet front offices routinely sign contracts that underperform — or miss hidden gems who deliver elite production at a fraction of the cost. This project asks: can we systematically identify which players deliver the most value per dollar?

Using a dataset of 120 player-seasons (2022–2026), I merged per-game stats, advanced metrics, and salary data to construct a composite Value Score — a normalized measure of on-court production relative to salary. Players were then mapped into four strategic quadrants: Stars, Undervalued, Overpaid, and Developing.

01
Data Collection
Scraped and merged player stats (PTS, REB, AST, STL, BLK, TS%), advanced metrics (PER, Win Shares, BPM), and salary data across 3 seasons
02
Feature Engineering
Normalized PER, Win Shares, and BPM to [0,1] range; computed weighted Composite Performance Score; divided by salary to yield Value Score
03
Quadrant Analysis
Segmented all players using median salary and median performance as thresholds, creating four interpretable strategic categories
04
Regression Model
Built a linear regression predicting expected salary from stats; salary delta (actual minus predicted) reveals over/underpaid contracts

Background

The Problem

Research shows that acquiring a new customer costs five times more than retaining an existing one. Yet most companies have no early-warning system for churn. This project builds a predictive model that flags at-risk customers before they leave, giving retention teams a data-driven playbook.

Using 1,500 anonymized e-commerce customer records, I engineered 15 behavioral features from raw transaction logs, trained and compared two classification models, and segmented customers into three risk tiers — each with a tailored retention recommendation.

01
SQL Feature Engineering
Extracted 12 behavioral features: recency, order frequency, complaint rate, cashback satisfaction, login preference, city tier, and more
02
Preprocessing
Encoded categorical variables, standardized numeric features with StandardScaler, performed stratified 80/20 train-test split
03
Model Training
Trained Logistic Regression and Random Forest; evaluated on AUC-ROC, 5-fold cross-validation, and confusion matrix on holdout set
04
Retention Dashboard
Segmented all customers into Low/Medium/High risk tiers; mapped each tier to actionable retention strategies

Risk Segmentation

Key Discovery: Cashback Satisfaction is the #1 Churn Driver

Cashback Satisfaction Score emerged as the strongest single predictor of churn, outranking tenure, order frequency, and complaint count. Customers rating satisfaction at 1 or 2 churned at nearly 3× the rate of those rating 4 or 5.

Recommended retention actions per risk tier:

Risk TierChurn ProbabilityCustomer CountRecommended Action
Low Risk < 30% 1472 customers Monitor via monthly email digest
Medium Risk 30–60% 24 customers Targeted cashback offer + personal outreach
High Risk > 60% 4 customers Immediate retention call + loyalty upgrade

Recency Signal

Days since last order was the #2 predictor. Customers inactive for 60+ days had a 2.4× higher churn probability.

Complaint Impact

Each unresolved complaint increased predicted churn probability by approximately 18 percentage points on average vs. ~6pp for resolved complaints.

Tenure Paradox

Longer tenure slightly decreased churn risk but also correlated with higher cashback expectations. Long-tenured, low-satisfaction customers were the most dangerous segment.
